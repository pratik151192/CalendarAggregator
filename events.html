Weiqi (围棋, the Game of Go), Sun Tzu, and the Chinese Way of War (10/24)

Title: Weiqi (围棋, the Game of Go), Sun Tzu, and the Chinese Way of War (10/24)

Start Time: 2013-10-24 - End Time:

Location: 113 IST Building, University Park 16802

Description: ABSTRACT Weiqi, or the game of Go, is a Chinese-invented board game. It is the world’s oldest board game; yet still arguably the most sophisticated and challenging one. In many ways, this game is a living reflection of Chinese culture, philosophy, strategic thinking, and operational art. Most sports and games find their origins in human conflict. They turn deadly human confrontations into regulated competitions. The Olympic Games are prime examples. Weiqi is no exception. Once created, sports and games also become part of culture. Culture influences human thinking and behavior in many ways. American football, for instance, finds its expressions in U.S. national security decision-making, diplomatic interactions, and above all, military undertakings. Weiqi as a Chinese cultural artifact is intrinsically connected to Sun Tzu’s Art of War and the Chinese way of war. The presentation on Weiqi, Sun Tzu, and the Chinese Way of War will highlight the fundamentals of the Chinese strategic culture, Sun Tzu’s Art of War, and uses Weiqi to illustrate the Chinese way of strategic thinking and operational art. DR. DAVID LAI Dr. David Lai is a Research Professor of Asian Security Affairs at the Strategic Studies Institute (SSI) of the U.S. Army War College (USAWC). Before joining SSI, Dr. Lai was on the faculty of the U.S. Air War College. Born and raised in China, Lai witnessed China’s “Cultural Revolution,” its economic reform, and changes in China’s foreign relations, the most important of all, U.S.-China relations. Dr. Lai will also be speaking at 6:30 p.m. on Oct. 24th in the Lewis Katz Building on the topic, “U.S.-China Relations and the Chinese Way of War.”

Teaching Technology for Social Justice, with Dr. Lynette Kvasny

Title: Teaching Technology for Social Justice, with Dr. Lynette Kvasny

Start Time: 2013-09-27 - End Time:

Location: 208 IST Building, University Park, PA 16802

Description: A George McMurtry Lecture ********************** ABSTRACT As an educator, my objective is to raise students’ consciousness of both the opportunities and problems that arise from our world’s growing reliance on information and communication technology (ICT). I do so by bringing a social justice perspective to the study of ICT and society. According to Adams, Bell and Griffin (1997), social justice education utilizes experiential pedagogical principles that help students to understand power relations expressed in oppressive systems that incite feelings of marginalization, exploitation of labor, threats of violence, cultural imperialism, and feelings of powerlessness. As students apply these themes to familiar ICTs and their digital culture, they become conscious of their operating worldview and are able to examine alternative ways of understanding the world and social relations. Social justice education has the potential to prepare students to become something more than technologists who are able to effectively design, implement and manage information systems. They are becoming people who are sophisticated in their understanding of social interactions amongst diverse groups, and are able to critique current technology arrangements and envision new possibilities for ICT and society. ABOUT LYNETTE KVASNY Lynette Kvasny, associate professor of Information Sciences and Technology, received her Ph.D. in Computer Information Systems from the Robinson College of Business, Georgia State University. Her research focuses on how and why historically underserved groups appropriate information and communication technologies. Lynette Kvasny is creative and tireless in her selection and integration of real world examples of information and communication technology into her teaching activities. In courses ranging from freshman seminars to doctoral research foundations, she guides her students to recognize and articulate how technologies that they often take for granted are having powerful effects on individuals, social groups, organizations and cultures.

IST talk: Informal Animation Sketching with K-Sketch

Title: IST talk: Informal Animation Sketching with K-Sketch

Start Time: 2011-05-31 - End Time: 2011-05-31

Description: Abstract: Informal interfaces capture ideas in natural expressive forms, such as sketching and speaking, to avoid the burden of specifying precise details. Animation is a perfect test bed for informal interfaces, because it is a powerful expressive medium, but current animation tools are either too restrictive or too complex. K-Sketch is a “kinetic” sketch pad for informal animation that allows users to create simple animations quickly and easily with sketching and hand gestures. To design K-Sketch, I analyzed 72 usage scenarios with a novel conceptual modeling technique called interface optimization. In this talk, I will present the K-Sketch interface and explain how interface optimization was used during the design process. I will also describe laboratory studies that show significant improvements over existing tools, show how K-Sketch is being used by both educators and designers, and explain how it is being extended to support prototyping of more polished animations and video games. Bio: Richard Davis is an Assistant Professor of Information Systems at Singapore Management University specializing in Human-Computer Interaction. The goal of his research is to create user interfaces that fit organically into human life and feel like a natural extension of the human mind and body. Much of his work focuses on creative tools for novices and professional designers. Richard completed his bachelors and masters degrees in Electrical Engineering and Computer Science (with a minor in Theater Arts) at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1995. He then worked on creative tools in industry (at Intel, mimio, BorisFX, and The MathWorks) before completing his Ph.D. in Computer Science at the University of California, Berkeley in 2008. He currently resides in Singapore. More information on his current and past projects is available at www.richardcdavis.com
